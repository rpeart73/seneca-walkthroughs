Week 9: Technological Benevolence: When "Help" Harms
Fall 2026

Purpose and Learning Outcomes

Week 9 completes the four dimensions of the New Jim Code with technological benevolence, the false promise of help. Students learn to look past benevolent framing and to ask whether a proposed fix actually addresses the harm.

By the end of this week you should be able to

  • Define technological benevolence as harm carried inside the language of help.
  • Recognize the benevolence trap, where a fix sold as care disarms criticism.
  • Explain Benjamin's argument in Raising Robots about helpers, disposability, and race.
  • Apply the will-the-fix-fix-it test to a real technology and add it to your Living Cartography.

Guiding Questions

Hold these in mind as you move through the week.
  • What makes a technology sold as help harder to criticize than one that is openly harmful?
  • Will the fix fix it? Pick a proposed solution and ask what it actually changes, and for whom.
  • In Raising Robots, why does Benjamin connect the disposability of robots to the denigration of racialized people?
  • Who benefits when a harm is reframed as a problem that technology has already solved?
  • How would you tell a real repair from a comfortable story about one?

Technological benevolence

Technological benevolence is technology sold as good for us, a fix, an upgrade, an act of care, that still carries the old harms. The benevolent framing is not a side effect; it is how the harm survives, because help is hard to argue with. A tool sold as the solution is harder to question than one that is openly harmful. That difficulty is the trap.

She gives the example of police throwbots, sent in first so officers can own the real estate with their eyes before paying with their bodies. The machine is framed as safety. Ask: safety for whom.

Key Terms to Know

Tap or click a card (or press Enter) to reveal the definition.
Technological benevolence
Technological benevolence is Benjamin's fourth dimension: technology promoted as good for us, a fix, an upgrade, an act of help, that still carries existing harms. The benevolent framing is not a side effect; it is part of how the harm survives, because help is hard to argue with.
The benevolence trap
The benevolence trap is what makes this dimension dangerous. A tool sold as the solution disarms criticism in advance. To question it can feel like opposing progress or refusing help. So the trap is not only the harm itself, but the difficulty of naming it once it wears the language of care.
Will the fix fix it?
This is Benjamin's working question for the dimension. For any proposed fix, ask what it actually changes and for whom. In Raising Robots she shows how machines framed as helpers, including police throwbots, can serve power while being sold as safety, and how the disposability of robots travels with the denigration of racialized people. The test is to separate real repair from a comfortable story about one.

Reflection Corner

The most dangerous systems may be the ones that arrive as help. If a tool is sold as good for you, as the fix, what makes it harder to question than one that is openly harmful, and how would you tell a real repair from a comfortable story about one?
