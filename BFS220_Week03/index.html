What Happens When You See Differently

In 2017, Jordan Peele released Get Out, and audiences who had never questioned representation were suddenly forced to decode it. The film did not introduce a new experience of Blackness. It made visible what Black audiences had always seen: the smile that conceals consumption, the liberal gaze that performs welcome while erasing the person it looks at. Peele encoded meaning that Black viewers recognized immediately and white viewers had to learn to read. That gap between recognition and learning is where Stuart Hall's work lives. This week, you learn to read that gap. Not just in horror films, but in everything you watch, share, and scroll past.

Insight

Get Out did not create a new way of seeing. It made an existing Black way of seeing impossible to ignore.

Stuart Hall: The Man Who Named It

Stuart Hall was born in Kingston, Jamaica, in 1932 and arrived in England as a Rhodes Scholar in 1951. He became the most influential cultural theorist of the twentieth century, not by studying culture from above, but by insisting that popular culture is where power operates most effectively. Hall argued that representation is never innocent. Every image, every story, every news clip is a site where meaning is made, and meaning is always political. His encoding/decoding model gave us a vocabulary for understanding why the same image can mean different things to different people, and whose meaning wins. He directed the Centre for Contemporary Cultural Studies at the University of Birmingham and transformed how scholars think about race, media, and identity.

Insight

Hall did not study popular culture because it was entertaining. He studied it because it was where power did its most effective work.

Black People Have Always Known

In 1919, Oscar Micheaux produced and directed The Homesteader, the first feature-length film by a Black filmmaker. He made it because he understood what Hall would later theorize: that representation is construction, and whoever controls the construction controls the meaning. Micheaux did not wait for Hollywood to represent Black life accurately. He built his own studio, financed his own films, and distributed them to Black theatres across the country. He knew that the question was never whether Black people would be represented. It was who would hold the camera. More than a hundred years later, that question still organizes everything in this course.

Insight

Micheaux did not argue for better representation within someone else's system. He built his own.

The Cost of Being Seen on Someone Else's Terms

In 1940, Hattie McDaniel became the first Black person to win an Academy Award, for her portrayal of Mammy in Gone with the Wind. She was not permitted to sit with her white co-stars at the ceremony. The hotel enforced a no-Blacks policy. She won the highest recognition the industry could offer for playing a role that reduced Black womanhood to servitude, warmth, and loyalty to whiteness. Hall called this stereotyping: the reduction of a person to a few fixed, essential characteristics that appear natural. McDaniel's talent was extraordinary. The system that rewarded her was designed to contain it. When Hall says representation is about power, this is what he means. The question is not whether McDaniel was talented. The question is what the system required her talent to do.

Insight

McDaniel did not fail representation. Representation failed her. The system rewarded her for staying in the frame it built.

Who Holds the Camera

When Spike Lee made Do the Right Thing in 1989, he encoded Bed-Stuy not as a crime scene but as a neighbourhood. The camera lingered on the block, the people, the heat, the radio, the arguments that meant something. Compare that to how the same neighbourhood appears in a police procedural: a location to be entered, managed, and left. The difference is not the neighbourhood. The difference is who is behind the camera and what they understand about the place they are filming. Hall's encoding/decoding model makes this visible. The same reality, encoded differently, produces different meanings. The viewer decodes accordingly. Lee did not represent Bed-Stuy more accurately. He represented it from inside, and that changes everything.

Insight

The same place, filmed by different people, becomes a different place. Encoding is not neutral. It comes from somewhere.

What Changes When the Image Changes

When Black Panther opened in 2018, something happened that Hall's framework predicts but rarely gets to celebrate: a dominant encoding of Blackness shifted. For the first time in a Marvel film, Black characters were not sidekicks, comic relief, or threats. They were kings, scientists, warriors, and spies in a nation that had never been colonized. The audience response was not just enthusiasm. It was recognition. Black viewers saw themselves in positions of power, complexity, and beauty that mainstream cinema had historically reserved for whiteness. Hall would call this a rearticulation: the same signifiers (hero, nation, technology, royalty) attached to Black bodies instead of white ones. The representation did not just include Black people. It recentred them.

Insight

Black Panther did not add Black people to someone else's story. It told a story where Blackness was the centre, not the margin.

The Photograph as Resistance

In 1942, Gordon Parks photographed Ella Watson standing in front of the American flag at the Farm Security Administration building in Washington, D.C. She held a broom and a mop. The image deliberately echoed Grant Wood's American Gothic, but with a Black cleaning woman replacing the white farmer. Parks encoded the gap between the American promise and Black reality into a single frame. The flag promises equality. The broom tells you what equality actually looks like for this woman. Hall's framework asks: who sees the flag, and who sees the broom? That depends on where you stand. Parks understood that a photograph is never a neutral record. It is an argument. And in 1942, this argument was dangerous enough that his supervisor told him it would get him fired.

Insight

Parks did not document reality. He constructed an argument about reality. That is what representation does.

The Most Consumed Black Canadian Image in the World

Drake is the most globally visible Black Canadian who has ever lived. His image circulates through music videos, album art, memes, and social media at a scale no other Black Canadian has achieved. But whose encoding is it? Drake controls much of his own representation, but it circulates through platforms, algorithms, and audiences he does not control. A meme of Drake does not carry the meaning Drake intended. It carries whatever meaning the internet assigns. Rinaldo Walcott argues that Black Canadian identity exists in a space of always having to prove belonging. Drake's global visibility does not resolve that. If anything, his representation is consumed internationally while the Black Toronto that shaped him remains invisible to most of his audience. The question for this course is not whether Drake represents Blackness. It is what his representation makes visible and what it obscures.

Insight

Global visibility is not the same as self-representation. Who consumes your image is not the same as who controls it.

One Gesture, Three Readings

In 2016, Colin Kaepernick knelt during the national anthem. One gesture. Three reading positions. The dominant reading said he was disrespecting the flag, the military, the nation. The negotiated reading said he had a point but chose the wrong venue. The oppositional reading said the anthem itself was the problem, that a song written by a slaveholder played over a field of Black labour was never neutral to begin with. Hall's encoding/decoding model is not an abstraction. It played out in real time across every screen in North America. The same image, decoded differently depending on where you stand. Kaepernick did not just protest. He made every viewer choose a reading position. And in doing so, he made Hall's theory visible to people who had never heard his name.

Insight

Kaepernick did not just take a knee. He forced an entire nation to reveal how it decodes Blackness.

What You Are Learning This Week

Four key takeaways from this module
1

Representation Is Construction

What you see in media is not reality captured. It is meaning produced through deliberate choices about framing, selection, and emphasis. Stuart Hall's work reveals the machinery behind the image.

2

Audiences Are Active

Encoding/decoding means you bring your own experience to every image. You can accept, negotiate, or reject the meaning offered. This is where your agency lives.

3

Stereotypes Are Power Tools

Stereotyping reduces, naturalizes, and fixes. It turns complex people into controllable categories. Hattie McDaniel's Oscar is the evidence.

4

Canadian Representation Is Not Exempt

Drake's global visibility does not resolve Black Canadian belonging. Multicultural narratives can mask representational violence as effectively as they can challenge it.

Key Terms to Know

Tap or click a card (or press Enter) to reveal the definition.
Encoding/Decoding
Stuart Hall's model describing how meaning is produced (encoded) by media creators and interpreted (decoded) by audiences through three positions: dominant, negotiated, and oppositional (Hall, 1973).
Representation
The process by which meaning is produced and exchanged through language, images, and signs. For Hall, representation is never neutral; it is always a site of power (Hall, 1997).
Stereotyping
A representational practice that reduces people to essential characteristics, naturalizes those traits, and deploys difference as power (Hall, 1997).
Misogynoir
The specific intersection of anti-Black racism and misogyny experienced by Black women in media representations. Coined by Moya Bailey (2010).
Africentric Lens
An analytical framework that centres Black perspectives, knowledge, and agency rather than treating Blackness as deviation from a white norm (Asante, 1987; Dei, 1994).
